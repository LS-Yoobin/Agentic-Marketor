# Photo Organization User Behavior Research

## Scale & Scope
- ~1.94 trillion photos taken globally in 2024; smartphones account for 94% of all photos
- Average person has 2,000+ photos stored digitally, growing exponentially
- Photos are the digital asset people hoard most and are least willing to delete
- 21% of adults feel overwhelmed by photo volume; women more so (26% vs 17% stressed)

## Common Organization Strategies

**Folder/Album Structures**
- Dominant pattern: chronological — `Year > Month > Event` or `Year > Event`
- Also organized by people's names, events, or occasions
- Spatial location used to indicate thematic similarity

**Tagging Behavior**
- Very few tags used in practice — even power users use ~20 keywords max
- Tagging is aspirational: people intend to tag but rarely follow through consistently

**Storytelling as Organization**
- Photos are mentally organized around narrative arcs (events, trips, milestones)
- Users prefer chapter-based layout over pure chronological grids when browsing

**AI & Automation**
- Google Photos / Apple Photos handle ~80% of casual organization automatically
- Facial recognition / face grouping is a flagship feature users readily adopt

## Pain Points & Failure Modes
- Smartphone photos are highly unstructured — auto-uploaded with no manual pre-processing
- FOMO drives hoarding: people keep everything including blurry/duplicate photos
- Photo hoarding causes anxiety, guilt, and reduced work efficiency
- Manual organization systems decay — people start but don't maintain them
- People are more detached from photos than ever despite taking more of them

## Key Behavioral Patterns

| Pattern | Finding |
|---|---|
| Organization method | Chronological folders (Year > Event) most common |
| Tagging frequency | Very low; minimal tags used even by power users |
| Deletion behavior | Rare — people avoid deleting even blurry/duplicate photos |
| AI adoption | High — users readily delegate organization to AI |
| Maintenance | Poor — most organization systems decay over time |
| Emotional driver | Memory preservation and fear of loss > active browsing |
